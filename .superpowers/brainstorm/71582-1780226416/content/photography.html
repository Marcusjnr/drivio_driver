A · Hybrid

Editorial portrait + cinematic urbanscape RECOMMENDED

"Portraits for hero. Cityscape for context."

What you'd see

  • Close-up portraits of drivers and passengers — lit, composed, dignified — for hero moments and brand campaigns
  • Wide cinematic shots of African cities at golden / blue hour — Lagos, Accra, Nairobi — for context
  • People sometimes in the cityscape frame, but always intentional — not candid
  • Warm rim lighting on portraits; ambient natural light on urbanscape
  • Shot list balances 60% portraits / 40% cityscape across the brand year

References: Cash App brand campaigns · Aesop · MR PORTER's travel features · Apple TV+ premium documentaries · Mubi

B · Editorial

Editorial portrait, art-directed

"All people, all the time."

What you'd see

  • Close-up portraits of real drivers and passengers, every shot
  • Warm directional lighting — rim light, soft fill — magazine-style
  • Subjects looking at lens or in considered moments
  • Restrained backgrounds, palette tied to Coastal Pulse
  • The face is always the subject — places become abstract

References: Aesop campaigns · NYT magazine portraits · Off-White campaigns · Vogue Africa

C · Candid

Candid documentary

"Real moments. Naturally lit. Slightly imperfect."

What you'd see

  • Drivers between rides, leaning on cars, talking to other drivers
  • Passengers waiting on corners, glancing at phones, hailing
  • Mid-action — getting in, paying, arriving
  • Natural light only, no styling, slight motion blur welcome
  • Frame compositions feel "caught," not posed

References: Airbnb "Belong Anywhere" · Lyft's early brand campaigns · Patagonia · Sean Tucker's street photography

D · Cinematic

Cinematic urbanscape, people in context

"The city, atmospheric. People in scale."

What you'd see

  • Wide shots of African cityscapes at blue hour, golden hour, neon-lit night
  • People present but small in frame — silhouettes, scale-givers, not subjects
  • Drama from light: rim of sunset on glass, headlight trails, neon glow
  • City as character — the scene tells the story, not the face
  • No close-ups, no studio lighting, no posed portraits

References: Apple TV+ premium docs · Spike Lee opening sequences · Sebastião Salgado · cinematic city photography (Christopher Doyle for Wong Kar-wai)

A note on the visuals

Each photo frame is a mood block, not a real photograph — the gradient, the composition hint (a portrait silhouette, a skyline, a candid figure), the color temperature. Once a direction is locked, the next step is a real mood board with actual photographs sourced from African photographers (Israel Andrade, Lakin Ogunbanwo, Stephen Tayo, Aida Muluneh, etc.) — but that's a separate research pass and lives in the spec doc as a deferred task.
